Abstract

We present the work of an ML engineer building intelligent systems that reach production. This document surveys three years of applied machine-learning practice spanning real-time computer vision, large-language-model safety, and retrieval-augmented systems. We describe deployed pipelines for live sports analytics at Liat.ai, guardrailing models that outperform published baselines by a 30% margin, and a novel retrieval-based method for adversarial attacks on LLMs, published as a peer-reviewed preprint. Across projects the guiding thesis is consistent: research-grade methods are only interesting once they ship. We report methods, systems, and results, and provide correspondence and supplementary material for the reader.

Keywords: machine learning · real-time computer vision · LLM safety · red-teaming · retrieval-augmented generation · production ML

Introduction

Machine learning has an implementation gap: the distance between a promising result and a system a person can rely on in real time. The author's work is organized around closing that gap. As a Machine Learning Engineer at Liat.ai, the current focus is real-time sports analytics [4] — systems that watch a match and reason about it as it unfolds. Prior work has spanned large-language-model safety at DMI Finance and retrieval-augmented conversational systems at Lilac Mosaic.

Projects

We describe three systems. Each is presented with a system-level figure drawn to scale.

RECAP — retrieval-augmented adversarial attacks

A novel approach to adversarial attacks on LLMs. Rather than searching the attack space from scratch, RECAP performs semantic-similarity retrieval over a curated database of proven-successful attack patterns, conditioning new attacks on prior wins. Given a target prompt embedding q, the system returns the pattern maximizing cosine similarity:

p̂ = arg maxp∈D  ⟨φ(q), φ(p)⟩ ‖φ(q)‖ ‖φ(p)‖

where φ is a sentence encoder and D the curated attack corpus. RECAP is treated as the flagship research contribution and is detailed in Section 4. Tags: LLM Red-teaming NLP Transformers. See arXiv:2601.15331.

CVAR — computer-vision assisted refereeing

A football-analysis system using YOLO and OpenCV for player, referee, and ball tracking. CVAR computes ball-possession statistics, per-player speed estimation, and camera-movement compensation, producing broadcast-ready annotations from raw match footage. Research

The flagship contribution is RECAP, a peer-reviewed preprint on adversarial attacks against large language models [3]. We reproduce its abstract and provide a citable reference below.

Abstract (RECAP)

Adversarial attacks on LLMs are typically discovered by expensive search. We instead treat successful jailbreaks as a reusable resource: given a curated database of proven attack patterns, semantic-similarity retrieval surfaces the pattern most likely to transfer to a new target. This reframing turns red-teaming into a retrieval problem, yielding attacks that are cheaper to generate and stronger in aggregate, and provides a growing corpus that compounds with every success.

The method is deliberately model-agnostic. Because attacks are conditioned on an embedding of the target prompt rather than on any single model's internals, the curated database transfers across model families. In practice this makes RECAP a practical tool for continuous evaluation: as new patterns are found, they are folded back into the corpus, and the retrieval front-end immediately benefits.

This orientation — safety as an evolving, data-driven practice rather than a one-off audit — connects directly to the guardrailing work in Section 2. Both share a conviction that robustness is measured, versioned, and improved like any other engineered artifact, and both aim to hand practitioners tools that keep working after the paper is published.
