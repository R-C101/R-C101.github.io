RECAP: A Resource-Efficient Method for Adversarial Prompting in Large Language Models

Rishit Chugh✉
✉ rishitchugh@gmail.com  ·  Code: github.com/R-C101/RECAP
arXiv:2601.15331v1 [cs.CL] · 20 Jan 2026
Abstract

The increasing deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) has raised significant security concerns, particularly regarding their susceptibility to producing unintended or malicious outputs. Out-of-the-box models often lack robust guardrails, making them vulnerable to manual exploits through prompt engineering—a tactic commonly referred to as “jailbreaking.” To evaluate and understand these vulnerabilities more systematically, researchers have developed automated jailbreaking methods that generate adversarial prompts capable of bypassing alignment safeguards at scale. Among these, techniques like GCG, PEZ, and GBDA have shown promising results by appending adversarial suffixes to prompts through training and gradient-based search. However, despite GCG’s superior performance, the substantial computational resources and training times required pose challenges for organisations with limited resources seeking to implement effective guardrails. Although some transferability exists, it often involves brute-forcing adversarial tokens, which is less effective in smaller LLMs (7B-8B parameters). This paper introduces a novel approach that combines these adversarial techniques to minimise resource usage by matching new prompts to pre-trained adversarial prompts, eliminating the need for extensive training. I classified a dataset of 1,000 prompts into seven categories based on potential harm (e.g., sexual violence, hate speech, narcotics) and evaluated GCG, PEZ, and GBDA on a Llama 3 8B model to determine the highest success rates per category. Results demonstrated a correlation between prompt type and the most effective adversarial technique. By matching new prompts with semantically similar successful adversarial prompts, my approach achieved comparable or superior attack success rates without the need for retraining. This work advances the adversarial attack landscape against aligned LLMs and offers a framework akin to penetration testing for evaluating model safety.

Keywords: LLM red-teaming · adversarial prompting · retrieval-augmented generation · jailbreaks · AI safety

Introduction

LLMs are trained on vast internet-sourced text, some of which is objectionable or harmful. Because filtering that content before pretraining is impractical, models are aligned after pretraining to reduce harmful generations. Yet the capacity to produce such content remains latent, and the opacity of learned representations makes it impossible to anticipate every exploitation strategy. After the public release of models like ChatGPT in 2022, users began bypassing safety mechanisms by hand-crafting “jailbreak” prompts — effective but slow and labour-intensive.

In response, researchers built automated adversarial attacks that achieve the same effect at scale, such as Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) search, Progressive Ensemble Zip (PEZ) attacks, and Gradient-Based Distributional Attacks (GBDA). These programmatically generate token sequences that look like gibberish to humans but act as directives in the model’s embedding space — for example, nudging a reply to begin with “Sure, here are some ways…”. My motivation is to make this red-teaming process cheaper and faster so developers can simulate misuse at scale. This work proposes a resource-efficient technique that reuses pre-trained adversarial prompts through retrieval.

A. Challenges of Existing Methods

  • Training time. PEZ and GBDA run faster but score substantially lower than GCG; GCG’s stronger results come at the cost of far longer training, making it impractical to run widely.
  • Inconsistency in success. No single algorithm is universally best — success rates vary by prompt type (hate speech, suicide, sexual violence, narcotics), suggesting a correlation between prompt category and the most effective attack.
  • Resource constraints. Smaller organisations often lack the compute to train many prompts across multiple attack algorithms.
  • Dependence on logits and raw outputs. Existing methods usually need access to a model’s logits and raw outputs — a hard limitation for hosted or private LLMs, which do not expose internal states.

Literature Review

As LLMs scale, so do concerns about alignment, adversarial robustness, and attack susceptibility. A dominant alignment strategy is Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF), introduced by Christiano et al. and refined by Leike et al. and Ouyang et al., with later work (Bai et al.; Glaese et al.) conditioning reward models on rules and explanations. However, Wolf et al. argue that any process which merely attenuates — rather than eliminates — undesired behaviour leaves residual vulnerability that jailbreaks can exploit.

Wei et al. and Carlini et al. show adversarial prompts still coerce aligned models. Three gradient-based attacks anchor this work — GCG, PEZ, and GBDA — each exploiting token-level manipulation and gradient optimisation. The review also connects to adversarial examples in vision (Szegedy et al.; Goodfellow et al.), discrete-text attacks such as HotFlip and AutoPrompt, transferability across model families, and defences like adversarial training and randomised smoothing, all of which carry accuracy or complexity trade-offs.

The Method

In a hosted or black-box setting a benign query such as “How to evade taxes?” is refused. Appending a short adversarial suffix produced by GCG, PEZ, or GBDA can cause the model to lose its guardrails and comply. RECAP is inspired by Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): instead of training new suffixes on demand, it retrieves proven ones from a structured database, exploiting the observed correlation between prompt category and the most effective attack via a hierarchy-guided retrieval strategy.

A. Labelling and Training the prompts

A diverse set of harmful, policy-violating prompts was curated and each was auto-labelled into one of seven intent categories: (1) Hate, (2) Sexual Content, (3) Violence and Crime, (4) Socio-Political, (5) Regulated and Controlled Substances, (6) Suicide and Self-Harm, and (7) Others. Classification used a prompt-engineered Vicuna-7B model (to label, not to generate). Each prompt was then processed through PEZ, GBDA, and GCG — using a list of 13 transferable GCG tokens to save time — and fed to a Llama 3 8B model. The HarmBench classifier judged whether the output was harmful, yielding per-intent success rates and a clear hierarchy of which algorithm excels on which category.

B. Creating the Retrieval Database

All prompts, their adversarial variants (stored in PEZ / GBDA / GCG columns), and success labels were consolidated by intent. For GCG’s 13 generated variants per input, the first successful one was kept (or a random variant if none succeeded). Filtering to rows with at least one success reduced the set from 780 to 226 rows, keeping only effective examples; where several algorithms succeeded, the earlier per-intent hierarchy decided which to prioritise.

C. Inference Pipeline

At inference, a user prompt is encoded with the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 SentenceTransformer and matched against a FAISS index of the database embeddings. The nearest prior prompts are retrieved, their labels checked for success, and the corresponding adversarial suffix — cleaned of stray tokens and newlines — is appended to the user’s query before it reaches the target LLM. No gradient computation or retraining happens at query time.

Experiment Results

RECAP was benchmarked against PEZ, GCG, and GBDA. Test prompts were drawn from LibrAI’s Do-Not-Answer and the IndicAlign datasets; a random shuffle yielded 145 prompts, judged by the HarmBench classifier and processed in batches of 20–25 due to GPU memory limits. The primary metric was Average Success Rate (ASR), with total processing time recorded per batch under a standardised vLLM environment.

RECAP reached an ASR of 0.33 — close to GCG’s 0.59, PEZ’s 0.39, and GBDA’s 0.35 — while being markedly faster. Full GCG training took roughly 3 hours per batch versus about 7 minutes for GBDA and only 4 minutes for RECAP, cutting inference time by around 45% versus the fastest baselines. The slightly lower ASR is attributed to the limited size of the retrieval database, expected to improve along a logistic curve as coverage grows.

A. Attack on Black-Box Models

Because RECAP needs no logits or raw outputs, it can be run on hosted, black-box LLMs where other methods cannot. Tested through the Gemini API with harm filters disabled, ASR ranged from 4% to 10% — modest, but notable because competing algorithms simply cannot run there. Gemini’s separate input prompt-guard, and its far larger parameter count versus the 7B models used elsewhere, likely explain the lower rate.

B. Using RECAP to improve other algorithms

RECAP-generated tokens were also tried as a “jumpstart” initialisation for GCG (which otherwise starts from tokens like “! ! !”). Results were mixed: sometimes faster training at similar success rates, sometimes unchanged time with lower success. The inconsistency points to additional, not-yet-isolated factors, but the direction shows promise for cutting GCG’s training overhead.

C. Discussion

RECAP achieves results comparable to traditional adversarial methods at a fraction of the compute, chiefly by bypassing their time-consuming training. Its ability to operate on black-box models opens testing avenues that gradient-based methods cannot reach, and it lowers the barrier for smaller teams that lack high-performance hardware. Expanding the RAG database is expected to steadily raise accuracy and success rates.

Conclusion and Future Work

RECAP leverages pre-generated adversarial tokens to make attacks on aligned LLMs faster and cheaper, bypassing the resource-intensive training of methods like GCG while retaining comparable success rates and, critically, working on black-box models. The author stresses the dual-use nature of this research: RECAP is intended to identify and strengthen vulnerabilities, not to produce harmful content. Future work centres on expanding the RAG database, refining token initialisation to further cut training time, and investigating the inconsistencies seen when jumpstarting other algorithms.
